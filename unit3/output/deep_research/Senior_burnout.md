# Preventing burnout in your senior year — concise, practical plan

- You're a senior CS student with ~18 hrs/week on your capstone plus other classes, research, morning workouts, cooking for your wife, and a 5:00 a.m. wake. - Below are prioritized, evidence-informed small actions you can try today, this week, and over the next month, plus capstone-specific workflows, sleep tips for a 5:00 a.m. start, household/time-saving ideas, campus support options, and when to escalate care. Quick prioritized small actions (small = easy to try and sustain)
- Today (doable now)
  - Book a single-session intake at campus counseling (many centers offer quick-access appointments).
  - Write one implementation intention: "If I sit at my desk after lunch, then I will open the repo and run the test suite for 5 minutes." Put it somewhere visible.
  - Create a 3-item "first tasks" list for your next capstone session (the tiny things to do first).
- This week (low-effort routines)
  - Start a 2–3 minute start ritual before each work block: open editor → run tests → pick one microtask. Keep it the same every session.
  - Try one Pomodoro-style session: 25 minutes focused + 5 minute break (or 50/10 if you prefer). End by committing or writing the next steps.
  - Leave a one-paragraph session "handover" before breaks: what you tried, what to test next, and 3 next microtasks.
  - Batch-cook one or two meals and freeze portions (30–90 minutes total) to cut nightly cooking time.
- Next month (build habits, tune schedule)
  - Fix a weekly planning cadence: 20–30 minutes each Sunday to pick 2–3 weekly milestones sized 2–8 hours and to block them on your calendar.
  - Implement a regular end-of-session 10–15 minute wrap-up (commit code, log blockers, set next task).
  - Experiment with one CBT-style technique (cognitive reframe): when you notice dread, write the exact thought (e.g., "I can't fix this bug") and list evidence + a small counter-plan (e.g., "I will test hypothesis A for 30 min, then switch"). Consider guided iCBT if helpful.

Capstone-specific workflow (to reduce dread and stuckness)
- Structure your 18 hours into repeatable blocks: prefer 60–120 minute deep-work blocks with short breaks. Examples: three 2-hour blocks across the week + two 3-hour sessions, or six 3-hour sessions—pick what fits your routine. Protect these on your calendar.
- Start ritual (5–10 min) for each block: open notes, run tests, re-open the top backlog item, set one clear 60–120 minute milestone.
- Break tasks into vertical microtasks (each doable in one block): e.g., "add failing test X", "write function Y", "update one doc".
- Debugging checklist (repeatable, reduces wandering):
  1. Reproduce reliably and note exact steps.
  2. Minimize to a small test case.
  3. Form one hypothesis and write a focused test or logging statement.
  4. Run, observe, iterate. Use git-bisect/logging when applicable.
  5. If stuck after 30–60 min, write what you tried, rubber-duck/explain to someone, then take a hard stop and switch modality (walk/sleep/pair).
- Re-entry tips after breaks: leave a "first 3 tasks" list; on return, do a low-friction warm-up (run tests, fix one failing test, or update docs) before tackling the hard item.

Beating the dread of starting (behavioral tricks)
- Implementation intentions: exact if–then plans dramatically help (example above).
- Microtasks: make the very first step 1–5 minutes (e.g., run tests or write a one-line failing test).
- Temptation bundling: allow a reward only while you work (e.g., a podcast you enjoy only during coding blocks).
- Accountability: arrange weekly 15-min check-ins with your advisor, a peer, or a capstone teammate; public micro-updates maintain momentum.

Sleep & 5:00 a.m. wake (practical, staged plan)
- Anchor your wake time at 5:00 a.m. daily (strongest circadian cue).
- Work backward for sufficient sleep: to get ~7 hours, aim for lights-out ~10:00 p.m.; for ~8 hours, aim ~9:00 p.m. Move bedtime earlier by ~15 minutes every few nights.
- Wind-down: start dimming lights and reduce screens 60–90 minutes before bed; use blue-light filters if needed.
- Morning routine to beat grogginess: bright light (outside or bright lamp) for 20–30 minutes, light movement/warm-up, and caffeine after ~20 minutes if needed. Allow 20–60 minutes before heavy cognitive work if possible.
- If you consistently can’t get to bed: try brief naps (10–30 min) early afternoon, not late day. If needed, consult a clinician about timed melatonin (evidence supports short-term use but timing matters).

Household and caregiving time-savers
- Meal prep: one weekly 30–90 minute session to batch-cook/portion meals; use one-pot, slow-cooker, or sheet-pan recipes; keep a few ready frozen meals for heavy weeks.
- Delegate and negotiate with your partner: propose concrete swaps ("I can handle cooking M/W/F if you cover X on T/Th") and trial for 2 weeks. Use specific language and measurable swaps.
- Use micro-sessions: convert small pockets (commute, waiting) to low-friction tasks (reading, triage issues).
- Hire/temporary help if budget allows (cleaning, meal delivery) during peak deadlines.

Using campus resources (fast, practical)
- Book a quick-access counseling intake — many centers have single-session slots or triage.
- Ask academic advisor for short-term options: extensions, pass/fail, temporary reduced load, incompletes, or medical leave—bring a short agenda (one-sentence problem + 2–3 concrete asks).
- Use Academic Success Center/tutoring and Disability Services for accommodations, time-management coaching, or testing adjustments.
- Join peer support groups (Active Minds, peer mentoring) for low-barrier social support.
- If you are in immediate danger or thinking of self-harm, use campus crisis numbers or local emergency services immediately.

When to escalate care or change plans
- If burnout symptoms persist for weeks, impair daily functioning, or you experience anxiety/depressive symptoms that worsen, consider ongoing counseling or psychiatric evaluation.
- If you are unable to meet basic responsibilities (sleep, eating, coursework) despite small changes, talk to your advisor about short-term academic protections.
- If you have suicidal thoughts or severe functional decline, seek immediate help.

Evidence notes and uncertainty
- Cognitive-behavioral (CBT/iCBT) and mindfulness-based interventions show small-to-moderate reductions in student stress/burnout in trials; effects and durability vary across studies. Organizational changes (workload, assessment design) also matter but are institution-level and less under your control.
- Behavioral techniques listed (implementation intentions, microtasks, Pomodoro, behavioral activation) have good practical support and experimental evidence for improving task initiation and short-term productivity; individual response varies.
- Sleep/chronotherapy advice is based on circadian science; melatonin can help short-term but should be used carefully and ideally with clinician input.
- Many studies call for longer-term and higher-quality trials; expect some experimentation to find what sticks for you.
